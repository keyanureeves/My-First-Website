My Blockchain Journey So Far

How it all Started

My journey into the world of blockchain began in early 2024 when I first heard about Bitcoin from a friend. Intrigued by the concept of decentralized currency, I started researching more about blockchain technology and its potential applications beyond just cryptocurrencies. At the time, I had no clear roadmap for learning but was eager to dive and explore this new frontier. At the time, I was coming from a background in Quantity Surveying , dealing with numbers, cost estimates, and reak-world assets. My work was practical and grounded in reality but I kept feeling that something was missing - especially when I looked at it from the perspective of how slow , opaque and inefficient systems around ownership, payments, and trust were in and around Real Estate or just in general.

The inquiry Phase

To be really honest, my initial thoughts on blockchain were a mix of excitement and skepticism. While I was fascinated by the idea of a decentralized system that could potentially disrupt traditional financial institutions, I was also wary of the hype surrounding cryptocurrencies. I questioned whether blockchain technology could truly deliver on its promises of transparency, security, and efficiency. However, my curiosity pushed me to delve deeper into understanding how blockchain works, its underlying principles, and the various use cases being explored across different industries.

At the time, blockchain felt like noise. Everyone seemed to be talking about crypto prices, NFTs, and how crypto is the next big thing. But I wanted to understand the technology itself and its real-world applications. I started by reading articles, watching videos, and joining online forums to learn from others who were also interested in blockchain. I quickly realized that there was a lot of misinformation and hype surrounding the topic, which made it challenging to separate fact from fiction. So I started asking questions:

  • What is blockchain beyond speculation?
  • Why does decentralisation matter?
  • How do smart contacts solve the problem of trust with code?

Most of the time early learning was research heavy. I spent hours reading whitepapers, watching explainer videos, and participating in online communities on discord. I also took some online courses to get a more structured understanding of blockchain technology. It was a steep learning curve, but I was determined to grasp the fundamentals. The concepts were excting but overwhelming - wallets, gas fees, networks, tokens, Web2 vs Web3 - it felt like learning a new language while still figuring out the alphabet.At this stage I was still a consumer and not a builder

Research Without Direction(and Why It wasn't enough)

For a while I was stuck in research mode, consuming content without a clear direction. I would jump from one topic to another, trying to understand everything at once. While this helped me build a foundational knowledge of blockchain, I realized that I needed to start applying what I had learned in order to truly grasp the concepts. Reading about smart contracts and decentralized applications was one thing, but building them was a whole different challenge. I understood ideas in theory, but nothing realy clicked. I could explain smart conrtacts , but I couldn't write one. That's when I realiszed something important: until I started building small projects, I wasn't truly learning and blockchain isn't a space you understand by reading alone - it's a space you understand by building, breaking things, and fixing them.

The Shift: From Observer to Builder

The real shift happened when I decided to learn how smart contracts actually work. At that point in time I had no idea where to start but I knew that I neede to get my hands dirty. So I called my friend , Ted and Sam who are both software engineers and they directed me to Web3 Clubs Kenya. Even though I had no computer science background, I knew I wanted to build something. So I joined there Solidity Learning Cohort and started learning the basics of smart contract development. The first few weeks were tough - I struggled to understand the syntax and logic behind writing smart contracts. But with persistence and practice, I gradually started to get the hang of it. I don't remember when but something flipped. Blockchchain stopped being abstract and become tangible. Like music. You don't just listen to music, you play an instrument. You don't just read about blockchain, you build on it.

Finding Direction Through Projects

Instead of trying to learn everything at once, I started focusing on specific projects that interested me. I built simple smart contracts, experimented with decentralized applications, and even participated in hackathons. Each project taught me something new and helped me solidify my understanding of blockchain technology. I also joined online communities and forums where I could connect with other blockchain enthusiasts and developers. These interactions provided valuable insights and feedback that helped me improve my skills.

I worked through Solidity fundamentals and applied them to real use cases:

  • A charity donation platform to understand transparency and trust
  • An NFT marketplace to learn minting, ownership, and transfers
  • Exploring tokenized real estate concepts, influenced by my background in construction and property

This is where my learning accelerated. By building projects, I was able to see how the concepts I had learned in theory applied in practice. It also helped me identify areas where I needed to improve my skills and knowledge.

I wasn't just learning syntax anymore. I was learning why the code mattered.

Expanding the Stack: Frontend & Full Systems Thinking

As I became more comfortable with smart contract development, I realized that understanding the full stack of blockchain applications was crucial. I started learning about frontend development starting wit HTML and CSS basic then later using frameworks like React and how to connect smart contracts to user interfaces using libraries like Web3.js, Ethers.js and Wagmi etc. This holistic approach allowed me to see how all the pieces fit together in a decentralized application.

This phase taught me something crucial: Web3 is not just about contracts - it's about complete systems. Smart contracts, frontend interfaces, backend services, and user experience all need to work together seamlessly to create effective decentralized applications. Understanding this interconnectedness has been key to my growth as a blockchain developer.

Where I Am Now

Today, my approach to blockchainis is very difficult from when I started.

I've moved from being a passive consumer of information to an active builder and problem solver. I now approach blockchain with a more critical eye, focusing on practical applications and real-world impact rather than just theoretical concepts. My journey has been filled with challenges and setbacks, but each obstacle has been an opportunity to learn and grow. I'm excited about the future of blockchain technology and the potential it holds to transform industries and empower individuals.

I work with:

  • Solidity for contract development
  • JavaScript and TypeScript for frontend and backend development
  • React for building user interfaces
  • Ethers.js and Web3.js for interacting with blockchain networks

An achievement that I am proud of is participating in Devpost's Build Resilient Apps with Polkadot Cloud and was one of the winners. That was proof enough that I can compete. I needed no more validation. I just need to keep building and learning.
